Scottish Climate Action Co-Benefits and Social Justice Analysis

TLDR

This project analyzes how the economic and social benefits of climate action policies are distributed across Scottish communities. By combining climate co-benefits modeling with the Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation, the analysis shows that while nearly all communities benefit from climate action, more affluent areas are expected to receive larger total gains. The project was shortlisted as a top-10 entry in a national climate data challenge.

About this project

Climate policies are usually evaluated based on how much they reduce greenhouse gas emissions. But many climate actions also produce broader benefits, including improved health, reduced pollution, safer streets, and economic gains.

These secondary impacts are known as climate action co-benefits.

This project explores how those benefits are distributed across Scotland. Specifically, it examines whether the social and economic gains from climate policy are expected to reach communities equally, or whether they tend to concentrate in more privileged areas.

The analysis combines modeled climate co-benefit data from the UK Co-Benefits Atlas with the Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation (SIMD), which ranks neighborhoods based on income, health, education, housing, crime, and access to services.

What the analysis shows

Several patterns emerged from the data:

Almost all communities benefit from climate action
Approximately 98.5% of Scottish neighborhoods are expected to experience positive co-benefits from climate policy interventions.

However, benefits are not evenly distributed
Communities with higher socioeconomic privilege are expected to receive larger total benefits on average.

Some specific benefits favor less privileged communities
Several environmental and health-related co-benefits are expected to be stronger in more deprived communities.

These findings suggest that climate policy may improve overall wellbeing but does not automatically produce equitable outcomes across society.

Why this project matters

Climate policy decisions are increasingly evaluated not only on emissions reductions but also on their broader social impacts.

Understanding where benefits accrue can help policymakers design interventions that support both climate goals and social equity.

This kind of analysis is relevant in many applied data science contexts, including:

public policy analytics
urban and environmental planning
equity impact assessment
geospatial and socioeconomic modeling
